organ-on-a-chip

Tissuelabs is a tissue engineering startup. The company's dream was to manufacture an artificial transplantable heart. Lucas proposed the first organ-on-a-chip produced by the company using stereolithography 3D printers. Organ-on-a-chip is an organ model that simulates activities, mechanics, and physiological response. The model has a 3D microfluidic circuit integrated with a cell culture. Each channel can represent blood vessels and other parts of the human body. For instance, an alveoli model is basically an air sac and a network of blood vessels around the sac.

Lucas was responsible for designing and testing most organ-on-a-chip and microfluidics 3D models at Tissuelabs. He developed more than 50 different models - used by scientists to conduct experiments and also used by the company to showcase the equipment's capabilities. The company's video shows some of these models.

Web cad/cam

The software has integration with slic3r and with the 3D printer. The user can convert different format 3D models - stl, obj, ply, etc - into gcode. The web app can 3D print with multiple bio inks and even mix different concentration of inks. Cell analysis

One of the problems the scientists were facing at Galy was to analyse the cotton fibers. They could not measure the cells because they were entangling around each other. A 2D image was not able to analyse overlapping cells. Lucas proposed the 3D scan of the cells analysis - an extra dimension can provide more information and developed A 3D scan method to obtain the point cloud. Lucas developed a VR app to visualize and annotate the 3D cell models. The app shrinked the scientists to a cellular level and enabled the fiber analysis from a close distance. The next step would be to create an AI to segmentate and measure the fibers.

AI cashier

Internship at Utokyo - Teeth Scan & Segmentation

FAPESP granted Lucas a scholarship to conduct a research on teeth scan presented at SIICUSP. Later, Lucas was invited to continue his research at The University of Tokyo (#23 QS ranking, 2019). Lucas studied CG, computational geometry, and mesh segmentation in Japan. The entire research consisted in 5 steps: teeth mold scan, point cloud processing, surface reconstruction, mesh processing, and segmentation of the teeth. The research was presented at ICGG and the paper was published here.

On the first step, the team proposed to scan the negative mold instead of the mouth scanner. The research used three distinct methods to scan the mold: a low-cost equipment called Horus that uses photogrametry, Xbox Kinect that has a depth camera, and an interferometry scanner. The interferometry scanner obtained the best result, followed by the Horus, and Xbox Kinect. We concluded that kinect is not suited for scanning small objects. During this step, Lucas studied about camera calibration, point cloud processing and visualization, and different scanning methods.

Secondly, the resulted point cloud was filtered and concatenated. Many diferent scans were performed in different angles in order to avoid any missing detail. The ICP algorithm that stitches common features from each sample joined the various point cloud.

Then, two methods tried to reconstruct the surface: marching cubes and poisson. Next, normal vectors in each vertex smoothed the reconstructed mesh and a region growing algorithm based on the curvature threshold is applied to the mesh to segmentate the teeth. The team assumed some heuristics to improve the accuracy of the algorithm. For instance, the program re-positioned the teeth to face upwards by calculating the principal components of the model and considered that the bottom part of the mesh is the gum.
