organ-on-a-chip

Striving to manufacture the first artificial transplantable heart, we created state-of–the-art organ-on-a-chip models that simulates the physiology and mechanics of real organs. I had the initiative of start developing these models at TissueLabs. I designed and tested more than 50 different organ models. Tissuelabs' video shows some of these models.

The model has a 3D microfluidic circuit integrated with a cell culture. Each channel can simulate blood vessels and other parts of the human body. I created the models based on organs and tissues from the human body using Fusion360 and scanning techniques to create the organic shapes. For example:

  • The intestine chip is a rugose duct (where digested food pass) wrapped by coil-shaped blood vessel.
  • The alveoli chip is an air sac wrapped by a dodecahedronal network of blood vessels.

I applied two different methods to manufacture the models.

  • Stereolithography: I used photosensitive resin and TissueRay to create complex shapes such as microfluidic systems. TissueRay is a bioprinter that has a LCD screen and a raising platform. This printer was designed at TissueLabs and built by me. I tested different parameters and shapes to ensure the organoids were properly working. I first tested the models with flexible resin and then with bioinks.
  • Coagulation bath: I proposed this method as stereolithography cannot produce multiple-material objects. I made a mold and printed with TissueStart the channels in the mold in a solidifying matrix. The mold enables the production of complex surface shapes. And, the extrusion printer can create complex vessel shapes and objects with multiple materials.

Custom cad/cam

To create complex shapes such as organ-on-a-chip, we need special 3D printers called bioprinters. We designed and built TissueStart (an extrusion printer) and TissueRay (stereolithography printer).

Besides helping with the mechanical design, I had a key role to develop a CAM/CAM to interact with the printers and create organ-on-a-chip models. The software was developed using Javascript and was hosted on the cloud, eliminating the need to install a software and solving compatibility problems.

The CAM software has integration with slic3r and with the 3D printer. The user can convert different format 3D models - stl, obj, ply, etc - into gcode. The custom CAM enables: the slicing of complex shapes with multiple materials, the control and calibration of the printer, real time adjustments during the printing process, and the use of the printer as an automatic pipette.

The software has an easy-to-use CAD tool that creates complex 3D organ-on-a-chip models from a simple 2D drawing. The CAD consisted in 2 simple tools: The first tool can draw the object contour. The second tool draws the vessels' path. The software extrudes the shape formed by the contour and creates channels based on the vessel's path.

bioreactors

The material used for bioprints are called bioinks - an extracellular matrix extracted from animal and vegetal sources. I developed two low-cost bioreactors used in the bioink production process:

  • Bioreaquel can mix animal tissue with decellularizing substances. The machine has two separate parts: the removable module made of PLA where the electric motor and all the electronics is; and the autoclavable jacketed flask whose lid is made of autoclavable resin, sealed with a rubber ring, and has grooves to attach the upper module.
  • BioWash can wash organic mass. The machine has a magnetic stirrer (powered by an electric motor) and a jar with an input and an output hose. A pump is connected to the output and ionized water is sucked from the input. The bottle cap that sealed each bottle is made of autoclavable material and prevents from external contamination. An arduino controlled the washing cycles by controlling the flow of water.

I was also responsible for fixing lab equipment such as bag sealers, magnetic stirrers, bioreactors, autoclaves, 3D printers, and centrifuges.

As a recognition for my proactivity and efficiency building and fixing equipment, I won the congeniality and achiever prize at the Q3 of 2021.

Cell analysis

One of the problems the scientists were facing at Galy was to analyse the cotton fibers. They could not measure the cells that were entangling around each other. A 2D image was not able to analyse overlapping cells and traditional measuring methods would break the artificially elongated cells.

I proposed to 3D scan the cells. Besides the scan method, I developed a VR app to visualize and annotate the 3D cell models. The app shrunk the scientists to a cell size and transported people to the microscopic world. The app enabled the fiber analysis from a close distance.



We tested different scanning methods: SEM, Latscan and the confocal microscope. The best results were obtained through the confocal with a proper staining protocol. To recreate the 3D model, I filtered and stacked each layer of the confocal microscope image, obtaining a point cloud.

Our team developed a self-checkout cashier wherein the client puts a tray with the products underneath a webcam, and the system returns the total price. To achieve this goal, we developed:

Internship at Utokyo - Teeth Scan & Segmentation

FAPESP granted me a scholarship to conduct a research on computer vision. I developed a teeth scan solution that was presented at SIICUSP. Later, I was invited to continue my research at The University of Tokyo (#23 QS ranking, 2019). I studied CG, computational geometry, and mesh segmentation in Japan.

The entire research consisted in 5 steps: teeth mold scan, point cloud processing, surface reconstruction, mesh processing, and segmentation of the teeth. The research was presented at ICGG and the paper was published here.


On the first step, I scanned a negative mold of the teeth. I used three distinct methods: photogrametry (Horus System), a depth camera (Xbox Kinect), and an interferometry scanner. During this step, I studied about camera calibration, point cloud processing, and different scanning methods.

Secondly, my algorithm filtered and concatenated the point cloud. I took diferent scans in different angles in order to avoid any missing detail. ICP - a function that stitches common features from each sample - joined the various point cloud.

Then, I reconstructed the surface using marching cubes and poisson mesh reconstruction. Next, normal vectors in each vertex smoothed the reconstructed mesh and a region growing algorithm based on the curvature threshold is applied to the mesh for segmentation.
